## 1) What did we ship that *changed the user’s nervous system*?

1. **Name the loop.** What is the exact cue → action → reward → relief cycle we created?

 - Cue: ______

 - Action: ______

 - Reward: ______

 - Relief: ______

2. **What emotion did we sell, not what feature did we build?** (relief, control, momentum, safety, status, “I’m not behind”)
3. **What became faster in their head, not just on screen?** (recognition, certainty, next-step selection, remembering)
4. **What did we make *feel inevitable*?** (“of course I’ll check this”, “of course I’ll log this”, “of course it’s up-to-date”)
5. **Where is the “upgrade” actually located?**
 - User skill increased?

 - Environment became simpler?

 - Anxiety reduced?

 - Coordination improved?

 If you can’t point to one, it’s probably vibes.

## 2) What did we not ship that made it work? (negative capability audit)
1. **What did we refuse to measure because it would poison behavior?**
2. **What feature did we kill that would have increased engagement but decreased trust?**
3. **What friction did we keep on purpose?** (confirmation steps, delays, limits, missing dashboards)
4. **What category did we avoid entirely?** (social, gamification, leaderboards, “AI autopilot”, public sharing) Why?
5. **What did we *not* promise?** The unsaid promise is often the real product.

## 3) What did the market believe—and what did we make untrue?
1. **Write the old religion as a single sentence.** “Productivity is ______.”
2. **What did incumbents optimize for that we refused?** (visibility, compliance, reporting, activity)
3. **What did we treat as a bug that everyone else treats as a fact of life?**
4. **What did we make cheap that used to be expensive?** (attention, recall, coordination, context, switching, accountability)
5. **What did we make embarrassing?** After using us, what behavior feels dumb to return to?


## 4) The lucky break (and why it wasn’t luck)
1. **What happened that we couldn’t have planned?** (platform change, trend wave, competitor failure, user story)
2. **Why were we *legible* to the moment?** (positioning, category name, onboarding, narrative)
3. **What did we already have built that made the break “catchable”?** (instrumentation, shipping cadence, distribution access)
4. **What did we do in 48 hours that others could not do in 48 days?**
5. **What is the repeatable version of that luck?** If you can’t simulate it, it’s a lottery ticket, not a strategy.

## 6) State-loss / duplication / latency / uncertainty (convert to a map you can instrument)

### A. Where do I lose state?
- What *specific* state? (intent, context, last decision, constraints, “why”, ownership)

- Where does it disappear? (tab switch, meeting end, Slack thread, after compile, after interruption)
- What do I do immediately after state-loss? (search, ask, re-open, procrastinate)

### B. Where do I duplicate effort?
- What do I recreate? (query, mental model, doc, task list, decision trail)
- Is the duplication **protective** (quality/safety) or **tax** (no memory/poor tooling)?

### C. Latency bottlenecks

- Waiting on: tool / human / permission / compile / meeting / “what’s it called”

- What do I do while waiting—**good parallelism** or **attention leak**?


### D. Uncertainty bottlenecks
- What uncertainty type?

 1) “What is the next step?”
 2) “Will this work?”

 3) “Is this worth it?”

 4) “Will I be judged?”

- What do I do to reduce it? (ask, research, avoid, overbuild, seek reassurance)


### E. Classify each bottleneck
For each item, force a label pair:
- **Structural vs Psychological**
- **Necessary friction vs Pure tax**

If you can’t label it, you don’t understand it yet.

## 7) Energy + agency truth test (turn “enjoy” into an operational spec)

1. **What part feels like play?** Name the micro-action (instrument, visualize, debug, simplify, narrate).
2. **What feels like gear-grind?** (meetings, polishing, stakeholder updates, edge-cases, data plumbing)

3. **Where do I feel agentic?** (“I can run experiments without permission”, “I can see impact quickly”)

4. **If I delete one recurring activity forever, what improves immediately?** Output? Mood? Velocity? Trust?


**Operational definition of enjoyment (pick one measurable claim):**

- “After 60 minutes, I have *more* energy than I started with.”
- “I voluntarily return to the task without external prompting within 24 hours.”

- “I generate new hypotheses while doing it (not just execute).”

Then specify:

- **Mechanism:** why it feels good (closure, clarity, novelty, mastery, social reward)
- **Where it harms performance:** what tasks get worse when optimized for enjoyment (deep work, careful review, privacy decisions)

## 8) Telemetry: the dangerous part (causality, ethics, product implication)

### A. The causality trap

- What relationship do you suspect:
 - exercise → focus

 - focus → exercise

 - third variable → both (sleep, stress, autonomy, deadline proximity)
- What would falsify your favorite story?

### B. If we detect states, what do we do?
Pick one and admit it:

1) **Nudge** (recommend action)
2) **Automate** (change environment)

3) **Notify someone else** (manager/team)
4) **Do nothing** (mirror only)

Each implies a different ethical posture.

### C. The surveillance question (don’t dodge it—bound it)
Replace “Is this surveillance?” with:
1. **Who benefits first: user or org?**
2. **What power does this create over the user?**
3. **What is the irreversible harm if misused?**

4. **What’s the “consent withdrawal” story?** (Can a user truly opt out without penalty?)
5. **What data would we refuse to collect even if it improved accuracy by 20%?** That’s your line.
